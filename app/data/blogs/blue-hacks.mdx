# Pitch Perfect: Lessons I Learned from Blue Hacks 2026

They say experience is the best teacher, but in the case of **Blue Hacks 2026**, experience was a very loud, very clear wake-up call. 

Going into the competition, our team felt confident. We looked at the grading rubric and saw that technical implementation and code quality carried the most weight. The "Pitch"? That was just a measly **5%**. As "hardcore" developers, we thought, *"Easy. We’ll win this on the 95%."*

But after 48 hours of high-stakes coding and a sobering final deliberation, I realized I should have listened to the veterans. They always said hackathons are 90% the idea and the pitch. I took that with a grain of salt then; today, I’m taking it as gospel truth. 

Here’s why our "perfect" code wasn't enough to carry the day.

---

## **The 12-Hour Argument and the JAR Protocol**
Our hackathon didn't start with an "IDE open, coffee ready" moment. Instead, it started with 12 straight hours of debating. We were so focused on getting the "perfect" idea that we spent half a day just arguing over features and writing down specifications.

Our project was **TsuperTipid**, a solution born out of the current oil crisis hitting our streets. We wanted to help jeepney drivers find the cheapest diesel prices along their actual routes. To make it truly accessible for drivers who might not have consistent data, we built the **JAR Protocol** (named after our initials)—a custom protocol on top of TCP that mimicked SMS behavior.

We spent hours:
* **Scraping real-time data** to ensure our prices were updated.
* **Engineering for low-bandwidth** so it would work even on "edge" connections.
* **Writing rigorous specs** before a single line of code was even typed.

We were proud of the technical "diskarte" we put into the JAR Protocol. We thought we were building the most robust tool in the room.

## **The "5% Trap" and the Lack of Resibo**
When we finally stood in front of the panelists, the 5% weight of the pitch felt more like 100%. We were prepared to explain our TCP logic, but we were completely unprepared for the marketing and persuasion side of things.

We realized too late that we lacked the **"resibo"** (proof). 
* **Zero Market Research:** We got too caught up in the technical implementation that we forgot to validate if this is what drivers actually needed. We had no facts or user interviews to back our claims.
* **The Sustainability Question:** When the judges asked, "What happens when the oil crisis ends?" we had no solid answer. We built a temporary fix, not a sustainable solution.
* **Marketing & Persuasion:** We didn't have the "convincing power" to sell our vision. We focused so much on the "how" that we failed to make them feel the "why."

---

## **When the Demo Fails**
To top it all off, the "Live Demo Gods" were not on our side. Our video demonstration—the one thing that was supposed to show off our 12 hours of architectural planning—refused to work. 

Suddenly, we were standing there with a technical masterpiece that no one could see and a business case that we couldn't defend. In that short window of the pitch, all the effort we put into the "95%" of the rubric seemed to vanish.

---

## **The Hard Truth: Communication is a Core Feature**
I’ve learned my lesson. In the Philippine tech scene, we often prize "malupit" (impressive) coding skills above all else. But Blue Hacks taught me that:

1.  **Validation is Step One:** 12 hours of arguing over specs is "sayang" (a waste) if you haven't talked to a single real-world user first.
2.  **The Pitch is the Lens:** Even if the rubric says the pitch is only 5%, that 5% is the lens through which judges see your work. If the pitch is blurry, your code will look blurry too.
3.  **Facts Win Arguments:** Engineering is about logic, but winning is about persuasion backed by research.

"Pitch Perfect" isn't a boast about how we did—it’s a reminder of what we missed. Blue Hacks 2026 was a humbling experience, but it’s one that has made me a better developer. Next time, I’m not just coding for the compiler; I’m coding for the user, and I’m pitching for the win.
